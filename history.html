           The history of linear algebra can be traced back to the Babylonians who, around 1800 BC, were known to solve systems of

            linear equations. Later, in the 16th century, Cardan developed a simple rule for solving systems of two linear equations with 

            two unknowns. The 17th century saw significant advancements, including the development of determinants by Leibnitz and the use of

            matrices by Lagrange in his work on optimization problems. Between 1800 and 1900, there were further crucial developments, 

            including the work of Cauchy on determinants, eigenvalues, and matrix diagonalization, and the introduction of vector concepts

            by Grassmann. The term "matrix" was first used by J. Sylvester. By the 20th century, linear algebra was established as a core

            subject in university curricula, particularly in the 1940s at American universities, and is now widely taught in undergraduate 

            programs globally.

            Linear algebra has a rich history, with its roots emerging from the study of systems of linear equations and determinants.

            Early developments in the 17th and 18th centuries focused on solving equations using determinants, and the concept of a matrix

            emerged implicitly in the work of Lagrange. The 19th century saw significant advancements, including the development of vector 

            spaces, matrix theory, and eigenvalue analysis.
